---
title: Tech Labor
---

Something I've been thinking about that I could use someone else's thoughts on.

What can "we" do in the face of the growth of AI. "We" in this case being tech workers (me in particular). "In the face of the growth of AI" being the fact that AI tools drive the value of our labor way down. They drives the cost of most software to near zero. Casey and Kevin on Hard Fork are calling it the "SaaSpocalypse."

For example I built BookSharePDX in 3 weeks, working 2-3 hours a day. It's a pretty decent, non-trivial piece of software.

And I talked recently to a tech worker who is facing corporate squeeze, worsening work conditions, greater threat of layoff. It's not something I'm experiencing at my own job, yet, but you've heard the murmurs: it's coming for a lot of us.

The bright side, the idea that I'm excited about: If the cost of software plummets then it becomes available to more people. More spaces.

I wondered: "what if DoorDash could be replaced with free software." Or Uber/Lyft, TaskRabbit, Amazon. Not so easy though. When tech is no longer the hard problem, then we get stuck on the actual hard problems. Online market platforms don't just provide software. They provide scale, availability, reliability of service, and risk management. Free services, non-profit, or volunteer-based projects consistently do not have scale, reliability, and risk management.

At Ameelio, I discovered that the hard problem wasn't software. It was political, structural. The prison industrial complex could not be simply torn down by a few excited engineers.

But. If all the tech *labor* is freed up because there is no longer a high price for software... *can* there be a change? What *else* needs to happen?

I've had the idea for a long time that "a little bit of coding skill" can go a long way in a bureaucratic process. I came up with it in 2014, when I learned from my friend Matt what he worked on as an administrative assistant. Painful to hear about. So easy to automate. I pursued this idea. In practice, it has only paid off a little bit, here and there, over the last 12 years. A little experiment with JOIN PDX in 2021. A little form-filling app for CLEAR Clinic in 2023. A little manual data entry task for CLEAR Clinic a few weeks ago. It took me 6 hours and it saves one office worker 5 hours of tedious work every week. Helpful, but not transformational.

EVEN SO. I hold out hope. I *want* it to work out, because I like writing software. In a way it's less fun than it was, but in another way it's also *more* fun to have 5x productivity. At least *I've* felt that way, though the information I'm hearing (from friends, from Hard Fork) is that for most software engineers it's much lower *AND* they often overestimate their own productivity gains. I think it's that it's limited to a small subset even of engineering.

But I've held out hope for a long time that this can be done at scale and transform systems... Maybe I'm wrong. Maybe the lack of payoff in 12 years is a sign that I'm wrong.

Ok. So. Tech is no longer the hard problem. Well, it was never the hard problem. But now we see that it's not the hard problem *even more so*. It still *feels like* there's an opportunity here. Still looking for it.

Ok, having written that out, I guess I'm bracing myself for a bunch of replies saying "yes, you're wrong."

Ok, on to the hard, boring problems then, I guess.
